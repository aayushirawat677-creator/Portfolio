Instacart — a Smart Batch Accuracy System.

Eliminating missing items and bad substitutions in batch grocery delivery — by adding the verification layer the shopper app has never had, plus an AI substitute engine that actually knows what each customer wants.

01 · The Problem

Customers expect what they ordered.

Missing items and unwanted substitutions are the defining pain points of batch delivery — consistently the top complaint category across Reddit, Trustpilot and app-store reviews.

In batch delivery, one shopper picks for 2–4 customers at once — which introduces real error risk: missing items (paid for, never arrives), bad substitutions (an unapproved replacement discovered at the door), and wrong-bag mix-ups. The downstream effect is severe: a customer who experiences a missing item is far less likely to reorder. Each missing-item event isn't a $2–15 refund — it's a potential customer lost.

This isn't shopper negligence — it's a workflow design problem. Shoppers juggle three customer lists in a single vertical scroll, with no color-coding, no guided bagging phase, and no real-time way to ask the customer what they'd want. The app ends at checkout; sorting is entirely manual — at the most error-prone moment, in a crowded checkout area.

02 · Root Cause

No verification layer between store and door.

Two failure modes, one structural origin.

// root cause 01

Cognitive overload at bagging

Shoppers manage 2–4 lists with no spatial or visual distinction, and the app gives zero bagging guidance. Items are sorted from memory → placed in the wrong bag, or missed entirely.

// root cause 02

No real-time substitute loop

An out-of-stock item is replaced on the shopper's own judgment; the customer is notified only after delivery. No in-aisle approval → an unwanted substitution with no recourse.

03 · The Solution

A three-layer fix — no hardware, no retailer integration.

Two workflow phases plus a personalization engine, all inside the existing shopper app.

P1

Consolidated Picking View — in store

inspired by warehouse wave-picking

All items across the batch collapse into one unified list, sorted by aisle/category — not by customer. Quantities aggregate ("3× Honey"), the app tracks attribution internally, and a simple tap-to-check (no barcode scanner) lets the shopper walk the store once without backtracking.

P2

Sequential Bagging Checklist — post-checkout

the core innovation — a new app state

After checkout, the app auto-transitions into Bagging Mode and shows one customer's list at a time against labeled bags (A, B, C). The shopper checks each item into the right bag; the order is fixed and can't be skipped. Any unchecked item triggers a red alert — "Customer C is missing one item — honey" — that must be resolved before bags are sealed. A forced completion check that catches errors before delivery, not after.

AI

Substitute Intelligence — when stock runs out

personalization that compounds

Marking an item out of stock activates an AI recommendation layer instead of leaving the call to the shopper's guess (detailed below).

04 · AI Substitute Intelligence

The shopper has no context. The model has all of it.

Bad substitutions happen because shoppers don't know what a customer values. Closing that gap is what makes a substitute feel personal, not random.

// signal 01

Order history

What brands, sizes and variants has this customer consistently bought?

// signal 02

Rejection history

Has this customer rejected a similar substitute before? Those options are deprioritized.

// signal 03

Preference signals

Organic-only? Budget-conscious? Premium-loyal? Inferred from repeated purchase patterns.

Every approve/reject decision retrains the model — at the individual and cohort level. Over time it learns that oat-milk customers want the same brand, or that organic buyers rarely accept conventional swaps. That's a compounding moat: the longer a customer uses Instacart, the smarter their substitute experience — something a competitor starting from scratch can't replicate without years of behavioral data.

07 · Launch Plan & Risks

Pilot first, then scale.

0

Research & validation · wks 1–4

Shadow 10–20 shoppers on batch orders, survey 500 customers on missing/sub frequency, define baselines from existing refund data.

1

Consolidated Picking View · wks 5–12

A/B test the unified list with 5% of batch shoppers; measure pick efficiency, satisfaction and missing-item rate; iterate before Phase 2.

2

Bagging Checklist + sub approval · wks 13–24

Launch to successful Phase-1 shoppers with real-time substitute approval; monitor refund rate, CSAT and checklist completion.

3

Full rollout · month 6+

Roll out to all batch-eligible orders nationally; surface accuracy gains in the shopper dashboard; evaluate Canada & France.

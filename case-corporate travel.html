Corporate travel — an end-to-end system design.

How I take apart an unfamiliar system: I pulled the thread on corporate travel — a product that looks solved and isn't — and reasoned my way to where it actually breaks. This is the architecture, the two flows that carry the weight, and where I'd focus if I were the PM.

01 · Why I did this

The part of the job I care about most: taking a system apart until I can see where it breaks.

Corporate travel looks like the most solved product on earth — you type a request, get a list, book. Then I started wondering what actually happens in those few minutes.

Where the price comes from, who owns the booking once it exists, what happens when the trip changes, and how many separate companies have to quietly agree for a single business trip to hold together. I've never worked in the industry — this is the record of an outsider reasoning her way in, which for understanding a system's shape I've come to think is worth more than pretending otherwise.

What I found wasn't a supply chain with tidy handoffs. It was a web of companies that all depend on each other and all, quietly, want to depend on each other less — and the technical problems are downstream of that. I wrote the full essay here →

02 · The thesis

The hardest job isn't booking a trip.

It's maintaining an honest, timely picture of a trip's state across systems the platform doesn't control and that aren't incentivized to keep it informed — while enforcing a company's rules deterministically and making the experience feel effortless.

principle 01

The incentive web is upstream of the tech

Content fragmentation, servicing gaps, and desync are the equilibrium of a system of frenemies — not a backlog of bugs.

principle 02

AI proposes; deterministic code enforces

The model reasons, ranks, and explains. Anything where one wrong answer costs money or strands a person lives in testable, auditable code.

principle 03

Prevent, detect, reconcile

Because the platform doesn't own the source of truth, its copy of reality drifts. The response is a discipline, not a one-time fix.

principle 04

The executor owns the transaction

One writer of booking state, so the source of truth stays coherent.

03 · The architecture

Six layers between the traveler and a world that won't tell the truth.

A request enters at the top, is understood by an AI reasoning layer, gated by deterministic policy, executed against normalized content, and recorded in a source-of-truth store that a reconciliation loop keeps honest. The red gate and the green loop are what separate a production platform from a demo.

05 · Flow B — the reconciliation loop

The half most first drafts omit: the platform is the last to know.

After booking, the trip changes at the source — and the platform is structurally the last to hear about it. This loop is how it fights back: detect the drift because nobody pushed it, then reconcile to the provider's truth.

06 · Where AI helps — and where it doesn't

AI makes the front effortless. It can't rewrite the core.

The whole industry is racing on the AI planner. Being precise about where the model belongs is the actual product judgment.

✓
Where the model earns its placeUnderstanding a fuzzy request, weighing soft tradeoffs, ranking options, and — critically — explaining a deterministic block in plain language, which keeps a blocked traveler inside the system instead of booking direct.
✕
Where it must never beThe enforcement gate. A spend cap that holds 99 times and clears a $4,000 overage on the 100th isn't a spend cap. A model is probabilistic — it always has a 100th case — so enforcement lives in deterministic, auditable code.
!
What AI can't fix at allThe core problem — an honest, timely picture of a trip — depends on parties whose incentives point elsewhere and whose plumbing for telling the middle of the chain was underbuilt from the start. No better model solves an incentive problem.
07 · If I were the PM here

Where I'd focus — the conclusions this teardown earns.

Understanding a system is only worth something if it points somewhere. Given everything above, here are the bets I'd make and the trap I'd watch.

Bet 01 · Fix content completeness before anything else

Leakage isn't primarily a policy problem — it's a "we didn't show the fare they found" problem.

The industry's own research points at the root cause: the top reason travelers book outside the program is simply better pricing elsewhere, and no amount of policy tuning fixes it — if the cheaper fare exists out there, travelers will find it. So the first bet isn't a smarter guardrail; it's the boring, expensive work of pulling in every content source (GDS, NDC, direct, LCC, OTA) so the managed path actually has what the traveler would find on their own. Completeness is the prevention. Everything downstream — compliance, duty of care, clean data — depends on the traveler never having a reason to leave.

Bet 02 · Make the compliant path the easiest path

Over-strict guardrails are a direct cause of the leakage they're meant to prevent.

Travel managers estimate roughly a third of hotel bookings already happen off-channel, and most report leakage flat or growing year over year. One too many legitimate bookings hitting a block or a slow approval, and the traveler stops fighting the tool and books direct — losing the company its negotiated rate, compliance, duty-of-care visibility, and data at once, often without anyone knowing the trip exists. So I'd treat false-block rate and approval latency as first-class product metrics, and lean on patterns like "show but don't allow booking" — let travelers see everything so they trust there's nothing better to find, while the program keeps control.

Bet 03 · Don't spend the AI budget on the core

Point AI at the front; don't ask it to fix an incentive problem.

The AI planner is a real edge — it strips cognitive load off the traveler and cost off the platform, and whoever makes a compliant booking feel like one sentence wins. But I'd resist the pull to throw the model at enforcement and reconciliation, where being right 99 times out of 100 is still a failure and where the real blocker is an incentive standoff no model can rewrite. The honest roadmap funds AI where it compounds — understanding, ranking, explaining a block humanely so the traveler stays in-system — and keeps guarantees in deterministic code.

The best product problems have a simple surface and a merciless depth — where every clean feature sits on a fault line drawn by incentives rather than code.

Corporate travel is the cleanest example of that pattern I know in enterprise software. That's the real pull for me: not that it's broken, but that it's broken in an honest, structural, load-bearing way — the kind you can't patch with a clever feature and can't solve alone.

MISO — the agent that decides what you can actually cook.

Not a recipe search engine. An ingredient optimization and ranking system: it reads a messy pantry, works out the best feasible subset, and returns the one recipe you're most likely to actually make tonight.

01 · The Problem

The fridge is full and there's still nothing to eat.

You get home tired. There's spinach, tomato, cheese, bread, half a cucumber, and one lonely brinjal. Twenty minutes of scrolling later, you order in. The problem was never a lack of recipes.

Recipe content is infinitely abundant and completely useless at the moment of decision. Three things break at once: decision fatigue (too many options, no way to choose), food waste (leftovers expire because you can't picture a meal), and low trust in search (results are too broad, too complex, or need six things you don't have).

So the job isn't to find more recipes. It's to answer two questions faster than a person can scroll: what can I make with what's here, and which one should I actually make right now.

02 · The Product Principle

Optimize for decision reduction — not recipe exploration.

This single line drove every design decision that follows, and it's what separates MISO from a search box.

// the reframe that makes this an AI product, not a wrapper
MISO is not a recipe search engine.
It's an ingredient optimization and ranking system.
// it doesn't generate recipes — it selects the best one for this person, this moment, this pantry

The consequence of that principle is concrete: show a few strong options, never a long list. A list is the problem restated, not solved. And when there's no perfect match, MISO shouldn't fail or interrogate — it should intelligently relax the constraint and pick the best feasible combination on its own.

03 · The Obvious Question

"Why not just ask ChatGPT what to cook?"

This is the first question anyone should ask, and a product that can't answer it doesn't deserve to exist. Here's the honest answer.

// the wedge, stated plainly
A general model generates a recipe. MISO selects one — from content real cooks have tested,
ranked for what's in your kitchen and what you've liked before.
// generation is a commodity now. Selection, memory, and feasibility are the defensible part.

I'd hold that claim loosely. The honest risk is that general assistants absorb pantry memory and personalization, and the wedge narrows. That's the bet: that "select and rank tested content for this person's kitchen" stays a different job from "generate plausible text." If it doesn't, MISO is a feature, not a product — and I'd want to find that out early rather than defend it.

04 · System Design

Parse → retrieve → optimize → rank → assist → learn.

The optimization layer is the interesting one — it's what stops this being a keyword matcher. The feasibility check is what keeps it honest about what a recipe will actually cost you.

!Structured ingredient extractionthe real bottleneck
The entire ranking engine depends on knowing a recipe's exact required ingredients and quantities. That's trivial for a structured recipe page and genuinely hard for a TikTok clip. So video is retrieved for the cooking experience, but a recipe only enters the ranked pool once its ingredient list can be resolved — from structured metadata, a linked recipe, or transcript extraction with a confidence threshold. I'd treat this as the project's biggest technical risk, not a detail.
03Ingredient matching + optimization layerthe hard part
Doesn't force every ingredient into one dish. Finds the best recipe-compatible subset — and is willing to drop what doesn't fit. Bread + cheese + tomato + cucumber work together; the brinjal gets left out, and that's the correct answer.

07 · Key Product Decisions

The tradeoffs I'd own.

Decision 01

Drop the brinjal — don't ask about it.

When the pantry doesn't map cleanly to any recipe, the easy path is to ask the user more questions. I'd rather the agent decide. It selects the best feasible subset and quietly leaves the odd ingredient out, because the entire product promise is less decision load, not more.

tradeoff: less user control, occasional wrong exclusion → mitigated by a single clarification question only when confidence is low
Decision 02 · revised

I killed my own hard 75% floor — and kept the principle behind it.

The original rule was clean: match score below 75%, don't recommend. But when I designed the actual screens, the rule produced an absurd outcome — a tired person staring at a full fridge, being told there's nothing to cook. The rule was optimizing for its own tidiness, not for the user.

So I changed the mechanism and kept the intent. The floor was never really about a percentage; it was about never lying to the user about what they can actually cook. A missing-ingredient tag serves that better than a silent cutoff: show the recipe, mark every gap honestly, and let the person decide whether four small items are worth a shop. Transparency instead of a filter.

tradeoff: more recommendations reach the user, some needing a shop → the honesty moves from a hidden gate to a visible label, which is where the user can act on it
Decision 03

Separate session memory from long-term taste.

If you reject one spicy dish tonight, MISO should stop showing spicy dishes tonight — but it shouldn't decide you dislike spice forever. One rejection is a mood; a pattern is a preference. Collapsing the two is how recommenders learn the wrong thing fast.

tradeoff: slower personalization → protects against overfitting to a single bad night
Decision 04

Retrieval never picks the winner.

The retrieval agent only builds a candidate pool; ranking is a separate, deterministic stage. Keeping generation and selection apart means I can tune what "best" means without touching how content is found — and I can explain any recommendation by pointing at a score.

tradeoff: an extra stage and more latency → buys explainability and independent tuning
08 · Guardrails

Where the model doesn't get a vote.

This is a system making daily food decisions, some of which can hurt people. Preferences are for the model; constraints are for code.

✕
Dietary constraints are hard, not weightedVegetarian, keto, nut allergy, low-spice — these are filters applied before ranking, never factors the ranker can outvote. An allergy is not a preference.
✕
Missing ingredients are always disclosedThe system never quietly assumes you have something. Every gap is labelled before you commit to cooking — the user finds out in the app, not halfway through with a pan already hot.
✓
One question, not an interrogationIf confidence is low, ask exactly one clarifying question or fall back to a safer recipe. The moment MISO feels like a form, it has lost to ordering in.
✓
Content must be usableFilter broken links, private videos, region-locked content, and spam before ranking. A perfect recommendation behind a dead link is not a recommendation.
09 · Measuring Success

One question outranks every dashboard.

// the north star
Did the user actually cook the recommended recipe?
// everything else — clicks, saves, session time — can go up while the product fails

Save rate and click-through are the seductive metrics here, and both can rise while nobody eats. A saved recipe is an intention; a cooked recipe is the product working.

But I can't observe someone cooking — so I have to proxy it, and be honest that it's a proxy. The chain I'd instrument: Start Cooking tapped → progression through recipe steps → completion of the final step → a next-day one-tap "did you make it?". Step progression is the strongest signal, because it's costly to fake and it degrades gracefully — a user who drops out at step two tells me something very different from one who finishes. I'd calibrate the proxy against the next-day prompt early, then lean on the behavioural signal once I trust it.

// the proxy chain, and its honest weakness
Start Cooking → step progression → final step → next-day confirm
// none of these prove a meal happened. Together they're the closest honest approximation,
// and step-drop-off is the most diagnostic thing the product can learn.

The supporting metrics all ladder up to that: time to decision (are we actually reducing the scroll?), selection rate, next-best acceptance rate after a rejection (is the learning loop real?), and low-quality recommendation rate as the guardrail metric.

10 · Scope & Risk

What I'd cut, and what keeps me up.

// MVP

Ship the loop, nothing else

Ingredient input, preference selection, retrieval across the three sources, the ranking engine, top-3 recommendations, like/dislike, and next-best. That's the complete loop — it either works or it doesn't.

// deliberately not built

Everything that isn't the loop

No grocery delivery, no meal-planning calendar, no computer-vision pantry scanning, no voice assistant, no multi-day optimization. Each is a plausible product. None of them proves the core hypothesis.

// biggest risk

Learning the wrong thing from one bad night

A single dislike is ambiguous — was it the spice, the effort, the cuisine, or just a bad mood? Infer too aggressively and the taste profile corrupts fast. This is why session and long-term memory stay separate, and it's the first thing I'd instrument.

!
Popularity outranking feasibilityThe failure mode that would quietly kill the product — a beautiful viral recipe you can't cook. Mitigated by making feasibility the dominant ranking factor at 40%, and by disclosing every missing ingredient rather than burying it.
!
The user feels interrogatedEvery clarifying question is a step back toward the scrolling it was meant to replace. One question maximum, and only when confidence is genuinely low.
11 · What I'd Do Differently

Where this design is still a guess.

The ranking weights are a hypothesis, not a finding. 40/15/15/10/10/5/5 is my reasoned starting point — feasibility must dominate, popularity must not win ties — but the exact numbers are guesses until real cook-rate data tunes them. I'd ship them as a config, not a constant, and treat the first month as a calibration exercise rather than a launch.

The thing I got wrong was the 75% floor, and I only found out by drawing it. On paper it was the cleanest rule in the design. On a screen it was hostile — it meant telling someone with a full fridge that there was nothing to cook. The lesson I'd carry forward isn't "don't set thresholds," it's that a rule which optimizes for the tidiness of the system rather than the state of the user will always look better in a doc than it performs in a product. I'd get to the interface earlier, precisely so my rules meet reality sooner.

And I'd define the eval before the prompts. The temptation with an agent like this is to build the pipeline first and figure out how to measure it afterwards. The thing that actually determines whether MISO works — did they cook it? — has to be instrumented from day one, because every other metric will happily tell you it's succeeding while nobody eats.

The taste model is useless on day one — and day one is when I win or lose the user. The whole learning loop only starts working once someone has rejected a few things, but the first recommendation is the one that decides whether they come back. So cold start can't lean on personalization at all: the opening rank has to run on feasibility, time fit, and source quality alone, and earn the right to personalize later. Better to be reliably cookable on night one than cleverly personalized on night ten to a user who already left.

And I'd stop claiming food waste until the product earns it. I listed waste reduction as a core pain point, but the ingredient-utilization bonus is 5% of the ranking — the system barely optimizes for the thing I said it solved. That's a strategy-execution mismatch, and the honest fix is one of two: raise the weight and accept worse taste-matching, or drop waste from the pitch and call it a pleasant side effect. Right now it's a claim the architecture doesn't back.

// what I'd do next

The first three things, in order.

This is a design, and designs are cheap. Here's what I'd do before writing a line of production code.

1
Validate the problem is real — I haven't yetI designed MISO from my own frustration and reasoning, not from user research. That's exactly the failure mode I'd flag in someone else's PRD, so I'll flag it in mine. First move: watch ten people decide dinner on a weeknight. If they aren't actually scrolling and giving up, the premise is wrong and no amount of good architecture saves it.
2
Prove structured ingredient extraction worksThe ranking engine is worthless without a reliable required-ingredient list, and that's hard to get from a video. Cheap test: take 100 recipes across video and structured sources, measure extraction accuracy, and find out whether the rankable pool is even big enough. If it isn't, this is a recipe-site ranker, not a multi-source agent — and I'd rather learn that in week one than month six.
3
Test the wedge against a general assistantPut MISO's ranked recommendation head-to-head with a ChatGPT answer for the same pantry, and measure which one people actually cook. If the general model wins, then the defensible thing was never the recommendation — it's the pantry memory and the taste model, and the product should be rebuilt around those.

01 · Intro

Answering two daily questions, faster.

"What can I cook with what I have?" and "Which recipe should I actually make right now?" — MISO narrows hundreds of links to a few you're likely to cook.

MISO is an AI recipe-recommendation agent for busy people who cook at home. Instead of forcing users to browse endless links, it surfaces a small set of highly relevant recipes drawn from trusted content sources — YouTube, TikTok and Instagram. The product is designed to reduce decision fatigue, save time, and cut food waste.

The core principle: MISO is not trying to generate a recipe from scratch. It's selecting the best recipe for that user, at that moment, with the ingredients available.

04 · The Solution

A faster decision layer on top of existing content.

Optimize for decision reduction, not recipe exploration.

The agent understands messy ingredient input, finds candidates across platforms, removes irrelevant ingredients when needed, ranks the best matches, learns from likes & dislikes, and recommends the next best recipe if the first is rejected. The strategy is simple: make the first recommendation fast, make it relevant enough to cook, and keep the experience lightweight.

Intent Parser converts natural language ("spinach, tomato, cheese, bread, 15 minutes, vegetarian, something tasty") into structured signals — ingredients, time limit, diet, preference — normalizing noisy input. The Retrieval Agent generates ingredient-based queries and builds a candidate pool across YouTube, TikTok and Instagram. It never picks the final recipe — it only assembles candidates for the ranking engine to judge.

06 · Matching & Optimization

Pick the best feasible subset — don't force a bad match.

The most interesting layer: an optimization system, not a rigid matching one.

Given a messy set — spinach, tomato, cheese, bread, brinjal, cucumber — the agent shouldn't cram everything into one recipe. It identifies the best recipe-compatible subset (bread + cheese + tomato + cucumber work together) and recognizes that brinjal is likely irrelevant to the best available recipe. Maximize match quality, but never recommend below a minimum feasibility threshold.

// match definition
Recipe Match Score = (available_required / total_required) × 100
// 4 required, user has 3 → 75%
acceptable minimum = 75% · below 75% → find a closer recipe, or ask one clarifying question

08 · Learning System

Feedback as multi-dimensional taste, not thumbs.

A dislike isn't a binary — it's a signal about why.

memory type
scope
example behavior
Session memory
Immediate re-ranking in the same cooking session.
User rejects a spicy paneer recipe → reduce spicy options now.
Long-term memory
Builds the user's taste profile over time.
Often likes Indian veg, quick recipes, nutritionist content → weights up.

When a recipe is disliked, MISO infers the reason — too spicy, too much effort, wrong cuisine, not enough protein — then keeps the same ingredient pool, downweights the rejected attributes, re-ranks the remaining candidates, and offers the next best match. That responsiveness is what makes the product feel alive instead of static.
